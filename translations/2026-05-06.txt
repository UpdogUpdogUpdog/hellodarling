A Certain Kind of Mood  
By Shigesato Itoi

・Yesterday, I met Moegara-san at "Kayo Kissa," and I just couldn't hold back. I had to tell him directly what I'd been itching to say: that he reminds me of the protagonist in the Korean drama I'm currently obsessed with, "Everyone Battles Their Own Worthlessness." It's not that they look alike, exactly. If you lined up their photos, they probably wouldn't look similar at all. But then again, when I actually searched for photos of Koo Kyo-hwan, who plays the lead, and compared them to Moegara-san’s, they weren't entirely dissimilar. And now that I think about it, there's something about him that also reminds me of Masayoshi Yamazaki.

It's not just the face or hairstyle—those might be important, but it's more about the "mood" they share. It's like an unspoken preference or a way they want to exist in this world. Although Moegara-san hasn't seen the drama, and of course, he neither confirmed nor denied the resemblance, I really want him to watch it. The only thing is, in the first episode, the protagonist, Hwang Dong-man, comes off as an incredibly annoying guy, so it might seem like “a worst-case version of Moegara-san,” and I feel a bit sorry for that. Even I started watching with some distance, thinking, "If someone like this were actually around me, it'd be a problem."

But the script is just so engaging. I should pick a scene without the protagonist to illustrate. Like the argument between a couple who are supporting characters. The husband, a film director, angrily asks, "Do you love me?" The wife, who is also the president of the film company (his boss), replies, "After 20 years of marriage, you’re asking about love? My heart wouldn't ache if I said 'no,' and saying 'yes' wouldn't bring any benefit. Think about it carefully," she says. The husband is left speechless.

I'm just continuing the conversation from "Kayo Kissa" here.

Thanks for visiting "Hobo Nikkan" again today. And so, the Golden Week quietly comes to a close.
