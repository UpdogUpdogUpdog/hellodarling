The Merchant's Perspective: Akinding  
by Shigesato Itoi

・"You should study more," or "All you do is play without studying"—adults have been saying this for ages. But now, I wonder, is that still the case? When a child is told to study, what are they supposed to do? Without knowing exactly what that means, children end up just pretending to study. If there's homework or workbooks, they might feel like that's what studying is. But when those aren't there, what should they do? Maybe it's as simple as just reading the textbook, or recalling what happened at school that day. Even coming up with those ideas is quite something. Just knowing what to do could mean they're already becoming a capable student, don't you think? Not that I believe children who are told they play too much are in the wrong. I understand that it's hard to distinguish between play and learning. After all, through play, we learn how to navigate life.

But back to the topic of "You should study." The phrase I often say, "Come up with more ideas," is quite similar. This one deals with thinking about things without clear answers, which might be even more perplexing. The best approach is to keep generating ideas, regardless of whether they hit or miss. But then, how does one achieve this? I've long been acquainted with this issue, and there's a book that guided me, sharing methods that I found quite insightful. I bought 50 copies through "Hobo Nikkan Itoi Shinbun" because they were genuinely useful. We even invited the author to be a guest at our Dojo. It's clear how you can constantly gather material. I began to read the word "sense" as "chance." In other words, it's a chance to sell, a chance to earn. It's about adopting the perspective of a merchant. I named this approach "Akinding."
