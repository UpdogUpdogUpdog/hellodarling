Don't rush to complete.

Shigesato Itoi

・What I'm telling myself these days is,
  "Don't rush to complete."
  At "Hobonichi," I often remind myself and others of this.
  It's not about giving up on completion.

  The vision of "completion" should continue to evolve, so if you rush to reach a "finished" state at any point in time, you end up wanting thorough preparations or exhaustive plans, and this can prevent you from taking that all-important first step. It leads to nothing but piling up difficult problems, worries, and empty boasts.

  Inside me, there's always been this urge to "rush to complete." It was there before, and it still exists now. I think I've written countless times as a way to keep this part of myself in check.

  At one point, I said, "Let's meet at a lower place." Communication, you see, starts from a lower place, where both parties are willing to concede a little. If you start with, "Two high-minded individuals have now joined forces," it often ends up as nothing more than pie in the sky.

  Even the phrase "Give your dreams arms and legs" embodies a stance that doesn't rush completion. Even if dreams are given limbs, they might only be able to take a few steps or carry light loads at best. Who knows what wonderful things might eventually be completed from there?

  I've heard that when young people set out to do something, the first thing they often do is gather sponsors (money). In such cases, they need to show a "finished vision," which pressures them to rush completion. It's not just young people; it seems like the whole society works this way.

  When you enjoy the process without rushing, both the circumstances and you change, and a path to a "new completion" emerges. After all, that's how human growth itself works.

Thank you for visiting "Hobonichi" today.
When we rush through things, don't we lose a bit of joy?
