There's no harm in just thinking about it.
Shigesato Itoi

・Calling it a thought experiment might sound a bit pretentious, but sometimes I find myself pondering things I've never considered before.

　For instance, here's something I often say in all seriousness: every year, I find myself working more busily. Back in my thirties, there was a time when I probably seemed like a "hotshot." During that period, I certainly felt busy, and I had a fair amount of work on my plate. However, looking back now, I believe that the amount of work from those days would be much easier for the current version of me to handle. This isn't boasting—just an observation about myself. Back then, there was an overwhelming amount of time that I thought I was "thinking," but in reality, I wasn't doing anything at all. I spent countless hours under the guise of preparing for work, but in truth, I was having fun and indulging in other enjoyable activities. It was a time of stealing moments from sleep to do a bit of work. Yet, now I understand that the foundational skills of who I am today were forged in those seemingly wasteful times. My habits and methods of generating ideas were developed during that era when I was, in fact, not really working.

　But after that, especially after starting "Hobo Nikkan Itoi Shimbun," much of my time was allocated toward more useful pursuits. Truly, every moment became a warm-up for some work-related endeavor, evolving into study sessions to make the work succeed. I constantly felt like there was more I could do, and I became my own taskmaster, driving myself to stay busy.

　And so, abruptly, we return to the "thought experiment" I mentioned at the start. For example, what if I reduced what I consider my work by 20%? Might my productivity increase by 20%? Is it too much of a paradox to suggest that reducing work could boost productivity? Yet, in this day and age, it's something I genuinely want to contemplate.

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nikkan Itoi Shimbun" again today. Does working longer hours equate to earning more? In the post-AI world, things might be different, right?
