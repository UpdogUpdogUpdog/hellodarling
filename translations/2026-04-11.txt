Mistakes, Even If They Are Mistakes  
Shigesato Itoi

・There are pieces of writing meant to make you laugh.  
  Sometimes, it’s obvious right away,  
  and other times, it takes a moment to realize.  
  It’s delightful to encounter something that makes you laugh instantly,  
  but there’s also a special charm in the kind of humor that sneaks up on you.  
  I enjoy both.  
  If possible, I’d love to read nothing but those kinds of writings.  
  Then again, too much of even a good thing might become overwhelming.  
  Writing, after all, shouldn't only be about evoking laughter.

So, let me flip the perspective.  
What kind of writing do I wish to avoid?  
Ah, the truth is, you can’t tell until you’ve read it,  
so I suppose that doesn’t quite work.  
Let me rephrase: what kind of writing do I struggle with?

(Here, I paused for a few minutes to think.)

I struggle with writing that, even indirectly, tells you to "hate" something or someone.  
It’s fine to point out when something is wrong.  
There are bound to be people whose views differ from your own,  
and saying "that’s not right" is perfectly within your rights.  
However, when you’re convinced you’re "correct,"  
it’s easy to spot "mistakes" in others and equate them with "wrong" or "evil."  
This is when things can get out of hand,  
and "mistake equals evil equals enemy" becomes a tempting connection.  
Add some fervor to that, and you start wanting allies for your viewpoint.  
You end up issuing commands or "directives" for others to "hate."  
Even those in power in nations do this endlessly.  
When you hear about "stoking division,"  
it almost always carries the message of "hate!"  
From international politics to neighborhood disputes,  
there’s plenty issued under the theme of "hate!"  
No matter how virtuous the person, I struggle with "hate!"  
A "mistake" might be a "mistake," but "hate!" is something I can’t handle.

Thank you for visiting "Hobonichi" today.  
There’s always the possibility that the "mistake" is on my side, after all.
