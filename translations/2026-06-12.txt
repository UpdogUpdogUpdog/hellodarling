Today, I found myself eating ramen once again.  
Shigesato Itoi

・I've always had a bit of a soft spot for ramen.  
  First off, I love the "information" surrounding ramen.  
  If there's an article about it, I’ll read it, and if an opportunity arises, I’ll visit various shops to try their offerings.  
  There are a few places I particularly like, so I go back to them, but if a new spot catches my interest, I'll make a point to check it out.  
  I also watch a show on BS-TBS called "Eating Ramen."  
  I have friends who are much more passionate about ramen than I am, and I enjoy listening to their stories.  
  While I savor the taste of ramen itself, diving into the world behind it is equally fascinating.

  Among us ramen enthusiasts, myself included, there's a tendency to view the current ramen scene like a "competitive sport."  
  The "stadium" is set as a single bowl, with the time to eat, average customer spend, and basic ingredients all defined.  
  The competition revolves around the noodles, broth, and toppings.  
  No matter how much effort you put in, or how carefully you select the ingredients, you have the freedom to do as you please.  
  If someone wants to risk it all for their craft, that's perfectly fine.  
  If the resulting ramen wins over customers, that's where its value lies.  
  Value is victory, and it's reflected in lines, reputation, and sales.  
  As the knowledge around ramen grows, so too does the number of self-proclaimed "connoisseurs" who believe they're experts.  
  This aspect might also resemble competitive sports.  
  Sometimes, customers themselves start their own ramen shops.  
  Just as strategies and tactics in sports become specialized, the method of making broth can turn into a complex art.  
  Some things can be understood through logic and science, while others seem like tales from an alchemist's book.  
  Either way, if it creates value, that's all that matters.  
  Reading about ramen is thrilling and engaging.  
  …Though, honestly speaking, even the simple "ramen" you'd find on a menu at a local Chinese diner is delicious.  
  It might not have any secret recipes, but if it tastes good, it's a win.  
  As a mere ramen-loving customer, I can afford to be carefree—sorry about that.

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nichi" again today.  
I suppose I'm more of a casual ramen fan, not particularly knowledgeable.
