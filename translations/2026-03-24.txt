Milk Memories, Trickling Down.

Shigesato Itoi

・If you were to ask ChatGPT about the trends in milk consumption in Japan, it would likely have an immediate answer. After World War II, there probably weren't many cows, and during the economic boom, there was a high demand for school milk—those kinds of statistics are surely well-documented. But when it comes to "milk" in people's everyday lives, I think it's better to ask humans about it.

In my own case, we didn't have milk in school lunches. We drank skim milk supplied by the U.S., and it tasted awful. However, at home, we had milk delivered daily. They'd bring glass bottles of milk right to the "milk box" every day. Memories of those milk bottles from that era are pure nostalgia. An old song called "Come by Bicycle" even mentions empty bottles.

In the drama "The Wounded Angel," which started in 1974, the protagonist (played by Kenichi Hagiwara) became popular for a scene where he drinks milk straight from a carton, freshly taken from the fridge. It symbolized the young people of that time—an incredibly convenient way to get nutrition. It was the "rational choice" of a young man who didn't care much about proper meals, the opposite of today's "gourmet" lifestyle, yet such a character was quite the heartthrob.

In the early 1980s, I invited a "one-man comedy" performer to an event. This was Denden, who has now become quite the seasoned actor. His act started with an unforgettable line: "Hey everyone, are you drinking milk?" asked with exaggerated flair. It was a skit questioning whether the audience was living a small-town, middle-class life—a lifestyle already becoming outdated. In this sense, RC Succession's song "Kimochi E" includes the lyrics "more than those who drink milk."

Come to think of it, around that time, I appeared in a "milk" commercial with the owner of the famous Taiyaki shop, Naniwaya Sohonten. These are just "memories" I've jotted down as they came to mind, but such things are unlikely to be preserved as information or data.

Thank you for visiting "Hobonichi" today as well. In the world of "hobbies," it seems memories will linger more than information.
