What comes next? I truly have no idea.  
Shigesato Itoi

・I remember learning in school that Gutenberg's movable type printing spread the concept of the "book," distributing "knowledge" to the masses. But realistically, not everyone could have easily gotten their hands on or read a "book," right? Sure, compared to handwritten manuscripts, printing allowed for hundreds of copies, but "books" were probably still incredibly expensive. And when you think about it, could the average person even read back then? It likely took centuries after Gutenberg's invention for "books" to resemble what we know today.

Once a "book" is published, however, what's written in it is set in stone. New ideas or stories have to come in a new "book." In contrast, periodicals like "newspapers" and "magazines" may not rewrite what's already there, but they can convey fresh information in step with the times. After all, the word for "newspaper" in Japanese literally means "newly heard." Even if the total volume of information isn't large, it can be lightweight and adaptable. With the development of transportation networks, newspapers could quickly reach far and wide. They could write, "This is how it was before, but here's how it is now," and this ability to update in line with societal changes earned them "credibility." Being able to correct mistakes became crucial.

With the advent of the internet, and now that everyone uses it, there’s no need for typesetting, printing, binding, or transporting. Weightless "information" can instantly circle the globe, and anyone can create something akin to a "book" or "newspaper." At this point, it's not about what can be "changed" but rather where to find something that "doesn't change." Searching for constancy has become more important. An overwhelming amount of "truths" are in constant competition. Deciding what is truly "correct" has become increasingly difficult. In the midst of this, AI has become widespread, offering guidance on "what is considered correct."

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nichi" today. With books, newspapers, the internet, AI, and our neighbors—all right before us.
