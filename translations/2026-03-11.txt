What’s all this about feeling powerless?

・2026 minus 2011 is, uh, 15, so it’s been 15 years already. I find myself doing the math just to confirm how much time has passed. Yesterday, a friend of mine said, "I thought we’d need to brace ourselves for a long haul, like 10 years or so. But that time has already passed, and it happened so quickly." There’s no TV news van parked in the hotel lot where we're staying, and there’s no crowding either. As we walk through the streets, we might recall, "This place was like this back then," and nod in agreement, "Yeah, it really was." But that’s only when we consciously think about it. Usually, it’s just an ordinary place where daily life flows by. The fact that we’ve almost forgotten how we wished for those days to return is, I think, a very good thing.

For 15 years, I’ve been coming to Kesennuma on this day every year. No one ever asks, "Are you going this year?" because it’s always in my plans. We just discuss how we’re going to get there. I come because I’m set to meet everyone in Kesennuma.

In the first year or the year after, even when I came here, I’d feel really down on the train ride back. I thought it was because of a "sense of powerlessness." The sorrow of feeling, "I can’t do anything, I haven’t done anything." I believed that was the cause of my gloom, but really, who am I to talk about powerlessness? Looking back now, powerlessness was a given. Whether I’m weak or superhuman doesn’t matter; what matters is that after 15 years, everyone has regained their daily lives. It’s something that powerless individuals, pooling their strengths, using their wisdom, machines, and tools, have somehow created—a "future." My own powerlessness is just a part of the ingredients that made it happen. I’ve come to see it that way now.

This might be the town with the most people I want to see. Meeting everyone in good spirits is the greatest joy.

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nichi" today. Not forgetting—that’s the one promise I’ve kept for these 15 years.
