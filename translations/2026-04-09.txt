A Moment with Only One Choice  
by Shigesato Itoi

・Today, during a conversation, we touched on something that often appears in dramas—a scene where someone meets their partner's father to ask for his blessing to marry. When the father asks, "Will you love [Name] for a lifetime?" there is really no room for multiple answers. Saying something like, "I can't predict the future," or "Love changes over time," or even "I intend to" would be completely unacceptable. The only suitable response is a prompt and firm "Yes." Trying to find your own "correct answer" only leads to a black hole of wrong responses. If you hesitate for more than three seconds—or worse, ten seconds—you're disqualified even if you eventually say "Yes." This is not a moment for thoughtful deliberation. If someone with a logical mind insists, "But I want to be truthful and sincerely honest!" they'd likely be shown the door. The world has these questions with no real options.

In sports, a world of wins and losses, there seems to be no alternative to declaring, "We're going to win." In fields where outcomes aren't strictly wins or losses, you might encourage risk-taking with, "It's okay to fail, don't be afraid!" But in arenas where results determine victory or defeat, I've never heard anyone say, "It's okay to lose." If you approach it thinking, "Losing is fine," do you really drift further from winning? Certainly, striving to win and constantly trying is part of the etiquette in competitive scenarios. And perhaps that increases the likelihood of winning. But is it truly impossible to take a bold approach where losing is also fine? There's always a chance of losing, no matter what. Sometimes, if you're sent off with a smile and a "It's okay to lose," perhaps that lightens the pressure and you perform better. It's something I wonder about.

Thank you for visiting "Almost Daily" today. To be honest, I can't say anything other than "We'll win" myself.
