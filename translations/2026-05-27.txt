The Essence of Sauce  
by Shigesato Itoi

・When hosting guests from abroad, it used to be common sense to suggest sushi or tempura as the go-to dishes. The guests, having heard of "Sushi, Tempura, Fujiyama," would eagerly say, "I want to try!" Starting with something like natto was never an option. However, as information gradually spread to our international visitors, dishes like ramen, onigiri, yakitori, and egg sandwiches have gained quite the following. It seems that during the Tokyo Olympics, held amid the pandemic, the charm of Japanese convenience store food was "discovered." The formal term "guests from abroad" has turned into the more lively "inbound." Whenever I hear that a movie superstar has been spotted eating ramen, I find myself feeling a peculiar sense of pride, as if it’s my own achievement, which is amusing. Lately, I've also heard that "tonkatsu" places are attracting a fair number of inbound tourists. I nod along, thinking, "Tonkatsu, of course."

With the situation evolving like this, I can’t help but wonder what might become popular next. I doubt it will be natto, but tofu, made from the same soybeans, is already well-known, and "edamame" has apparently become a common noun. Yet, natto seems like a tough sell. Since predictions are futile, I’ll go with my personal preference. Personally, I want to advocate for the vast universe of "sauce flavor." It's part of the fried food team like tonkatsu but still seems underrepresented. There's the non-Chinese style "sauce yakisoba," and the allure of "konamon" surely lies in its "sauce flavor." When dining with foreign friends, subtly steer them towards "sauce-flavored" dishes. There's no need to ponder what delightful outcomes might follow.

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nikkan Itoi Shinbun" today. The taste and aroma of sauce are even more varied than wine.
