Failure and serendipity are the ingredients of hope.  
-Shigesato Itoi

I grew up with the mantra "better safe than sorry." No matter the generation, parents and teachers probably said something like, "You may think everything's fine, but you never know what might happen. Consider every possibility, prepare for anything, and you'll have nothing to worry about." Raised with this mindset, I feel like I've limited my own potential.

Considering every possibility as a way to prepare isn't necessarily a bad approach to avoiding failure. In fields like medicine, railways, aviation, and accounting, it's crucial to think this way. However, if everyone adopts this mindset in every situation, it leads to excessive tension and clouds judgment, blurring what truly matters.

It's a bit late to be saying this, but I want to remind myself and others that a "good decision" isn't about considering every possibility or being overly cautious. Especially in today's world, AI handles that better than we do. What's more important is not forgetting what's truly important, making decisions even when they're not perfect, and not procrastinating too much. These are the lessons I believe parents and teachers should pass on.

In the real world, where people live and breathe, doing more often yields greater rewards. In this context, failure and chance encounters become valuable "ingredients." Rather than being overly cautious to avoid mistakes or relying solely on logical explanations, the encounters and experiences gained through action—failures and coincidences included—hold far more value, don't you think?

I'm constantly oscillating between the lessons taught by the adults of my past and my current self as I move toward the future.
