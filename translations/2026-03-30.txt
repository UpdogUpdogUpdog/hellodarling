Desire Over Prediction  
Shigesato Itoi

・Let me start with a simple example.  
Every spring, we have what’s called the "Pro Baseball Ranking Predictions."  
Both the Central League and the Pacific League have six teams each, and the goal is to predict how they'll rank by the end of the season. These predictions are quite tricky. Critics and experts make their predictions, of course, but they tend to show a bit of favoritism towards the teams they once played for.  

On the other hand, amateur fans also make predictions, but they can't help but let their hopes sneak in. Because they want their favorite team to win, they twist various factors to suit their desires, aiming to predict a victory (or at least a high rank) for their team. After all, what fun is it without that kind of emotion?  

Then, about half a year later, autumn rolls around, and the results are in. Here, an important point emerges: even though it's called a ranking prediction—experts aside—fans aren't thrilled if their predictions are spot on. Saying something like, "See, just as I predicted, our team finished last!" is utterly meaningless and just plain foolish.

Now, moving on to something entirely different from pro baseball rankings, aren't we surrounded by countless pressing issues these days, no matter where we look? Experts and insiders provide their analyses. Even those of us who aren't specialists try to think, research, and understand. We get swayed by various predictions, declarations, and loud opinions, with optimistic and pessimistic views mixing in. We rack our brains, imagining what might happen.  

But in reality, even if an unwelcome prediction comes true, it's not something to be happy about. When the worst possible outcome affects many people, boasting, "See, I told you so," serves no purpose. Most predictions, including those from experts, tend to miss the mark anyway.  

Ultimately, it’s more important to consider what we wish would happen.
