Confessions of a Nail Clipper Enthusiast  
Shigesato Itoi

・For a long time now, I've been deeply absorbed in the world of nail clippers. I've pondered over what makes a good one, purchasing both domestic and imported varieties. I've clipped, tested, continued using them, recommended them to others, and sometimes even gifted them. I've bought multiple of the same model, confidently declaring, "This is the best nail clipper in the world." Yet, I never gave up the search, always wondering if there might be an even better one out there. Because of this relentless quest, I've seen my "best nail clipper" title pass through several champions over the years, constantly searching, much like Masayoshi Yamazaki wandering through Sakuragicho.

A good nail clipper, of course, boasts sharp blades. You place your nail between these sharp edges to cut, and one might wonder, wouldn't the blades collide and dull each other? But that's not the case. The design ensures that the blades don't clash head-on; instead, they are slightly misaligned to glide past one another, truly cutting the nail. And so, I've been chasing after the very essence of this tool called the nail clipper.

But now, unexpectedly, I've been led to question my method of "cutting" nails. Have I been doing it wrong all along? You see, I've always been one for deep cuts. My nails have never been visible from the palm side of my fingers. I've trimmed them to match the curve of my fingertips, like this: "(". But then I came across a theory that challenged this. The "(", they said, was incorrect. It should be more like "[", a straighter cut without digging into the sides. What?! For nearly three-quarters of a century, I've been doing the "(", not to brag. While I might stubbornly defend my ways, I am always a seeker in the art of nail clipping. So, off I went to purchase a clipper that cuts in a straight line. I tried it out, but it was a disaster, an inconvenience in my daily life. The edges of my nails awkwardly snagged on the world. Perhaps it's just unfamiliarity, but for the past two weeks, I've been struggling with it. I'm tempted to give up on the "[", yet I yearn to explore further, leading me to purchase a nipper-style clipper that promises a perfect "[" cut.

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nichi" today. I'll give this a little more time, but I suspect I'll soon return to my familiar, rounded deep cuts.
