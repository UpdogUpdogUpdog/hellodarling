・Over at Shibuya PARCO, we've got a long-running event centered around Golden Week, and it's finally kicked off. At this point, I feel like I've become the unofficial host of this "Kayōkyoku Café."

I haven't planned out exactly what I'm going to talk about. I trust that the "charm" embedded in each song will provide the words for the audience. I'm not entirely sure how to define kayōkyoku, but I suppose it's pop music. Still, I believe it's "songs for grown-ups." Sure, there are wholesome, healthy, and uplifting kayōkyoku songs, but I feel they truly belong to the genre only when they embody the essence of adulthood. Even when singing about the innocent heart of a young girl or the spirited resolve of youth to brighten the world, these are crafted as "songs for grown-ups."

This time, I've been somewhat cautious with my song choices, but I think I've generally gathered "songs for grown-ups." They're not blunt, but they speak to the adult heart. Listening to these kinds of songs as a child, not quite understanding them, left a lasting impact on my personal development.

In the kayōkyoku of the past, there was this "seeping darkness." It was like a shadowy, passionate yearning that wafted from the adults. It was unsettling when I was young; there were times I wanted to keep my distance. But once you start to understand things, it becomes fascinating.
