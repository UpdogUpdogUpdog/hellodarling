Not everything needs a "review."

・I wonder if most people living on this Earth, when they eat something, see something, or hear something, don't actually "review" it. I'm pretty sure that's the case for me too. I don't always feel the need to voice an opinion or critique every single thing I experience. For myself, I think it's not that frequent. Then again, I can't say it never happens. In fact, maybe it happens more often than I'd like to admit.

I'd like to change this habit of "reviewing" everything. Whether I'm praising or criticizing, why do I feel the need to review so much? It's not just me, though. In the world of the internet, there are so many people who are constantly "reviewing." Everyone's starting to sound like those food critics from the TV show, always ready with an opinion. Perhaps that's why the phrase "looking down on others" was coined. Music gets reviewed intensely, doesn't it? I used to think soccer was particularly prone to this, but now even baseball has become a hotbed for reviews.

Kids, on the other hand, don't "review" much. If they don't like the taste, they either refuse to eat it or endure it without much fuss. They don't get into complicated reviews. If they're bored when taken out to play, they just fall asleep. If they're having fun, they keep playing endlessly. Dogs are like that too, aren't they?

I suspect that "reviewing" might be a very "cerebral pleasure." Maybe it's the current trend of "verbalizing" everything. You take an experience and translate it into words, allowing for more complex thought and deeper exploration. It's probably a "smart" kind of enjoyment. Kids, dogs, and my own absent-minded self—we don't really "review" things. I suppose having both approaches is fine, though.

Thank you for visiting "Almost Daily" again today. Personally, I'm aiming to keep my "reviews" to a minimum.
