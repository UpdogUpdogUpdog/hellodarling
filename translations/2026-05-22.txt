A Wordless Peace  
Shigesato Itoi

・Imagine a scene, perhaps at a daycare, a nursery, or a park. It's a familiar sight—little toddlers, almost like tiny babies, playing together, blissfully happy. Of course, they might not always be getting along, and sometimes they seem to ignore each other entirely. In that sense, you might not even call it playing. Oh, and it's the same with dogs at a dog park. They might chase each other, sit alone, or just play around in various ways.

Either way, there's this natural gathering in a not-so-spacious area. There's no coordination, no apparent purpose. And notably, there's no so-called "language" involved at all (well, there's the occasional cooing or barking). Each one might be hungry or sleepy, and there might even be little skirmishes. They're all separate individuals—individual dogs, if you will. Yet somehow, they all just naturally exist there.

I really enjoy watching scenes like that. It's not just me; humans, in general, might enjoy watching such things. I understand that in a world with objectives, structures, rules, strategies, and tactics, there's an interest in the game aspect of it all. That's why we watch dramas and sports matches. But there's something surprisingly fascinating and heartwarming about "living beings just being there together." It evokes a sense of longing. I'm particularly envious of the absence of "words." I'm supposed to be someone who's worked with "words," and I should be well-acquainted with their value and power. Even this piece is written with "words." But perhaps that's exactly why I long for "silence." Whether it's dogs or babies, they can communicate just with their eyes. Sometimes they leap or snuggle up. These "wordless" beings are irresistibly endearing.

Thank you again for visiting "Hobo Nikkan." Words can be weapons or the fateful red thread that connects us. But so can silence.
