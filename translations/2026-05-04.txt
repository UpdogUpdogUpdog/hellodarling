With the long holiday upon us, I find myself with time on my hands.

During these holidays, there's no strict schedule dictating "what's next?" It's a wonderful thing. This kind of nameless time, which we spend somewhat aimlessly, is incredibly important. Yet, at the same time, it also means that it's easy to dwell on things we'd rather not think about. Having just said goodbye to a friend, my thoughts naturally drift in that direction.

People often say that the loss of someone close manifests more as loneliness than pure sadness. When you're caught up in daily work, there's no room to feel lonely, and in a way, that's a relief. Although I can't blame the holidays, having more free time means I tend to feel less energetic. In this context, the "Kayo Cafe" at Shibuya Parco is a welcome distraction. There, surrounded by the flow of old pop songs, you can do whatever there is to do within that time frame. Even if it looks like you're just dawdling, you're not truly idle.

On May 4th, it's my turn to give a talk, so I won't be able to simply enjoy myself from the audience, but I'll make sure to have fun. Even for an old-timer like me, some of the songs they play are quite ancient, so the audience might want to brace themselves. I can't help but think how great it would be if Eiichi Ohtaki were around to chat with. But he's another one who's gone to the other side.

Suddenly, I find myself wondering about the word "believe." Where and how is it used these days? Is it treated like some baseless sentiment, as in, "that's just what you believe"? You know, unrelated to evidence or data. Modern times have pushed the religious—belief—part of human culture way into the margins. I'd like to reconsider this old-fashioned word, "believe." It's a big topic, so it's hard to even start writing about it.

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nichi" again today. With little people all across Japan having time on their hands, it might be a challenging period.
