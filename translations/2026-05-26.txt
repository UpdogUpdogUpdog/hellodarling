Manzai and Fiction  
by Shigesato Itoi

・The art of manzai, a form of Japanese comedy, holds a distinct advantage in today's world: the presence of a partner ready to deliver the "tsukkomi" or punchline. This allows the person playing the "boke" or fool to say the most outrageous, silly, irresponsible, or even dangerous things. Their partner, by providing a corrective interjection like "No way!" brings the dialogue back in line with societal norms. Even if one half of the duo says something incorrect, the team as a whole can claim adherence to social rules. This setup seems particularly suited to our times, where opinions and critiques assault us from every angle.

If a solo performer were to make the same remarks on stage, those words would be directly received as "his opinion" and might even be criticized as "his mistake." When you think about it, comedic talk is meant to entertain, not to express personal views. Yet, because it aligns with the speaker's own voice, people often equate the comedian's act with their personal beliefs. However, sticking solely to conventional thoughts won't ever produce laughter. In this sense, the manzai format—comedy with built-in self-critique—fits well in today's world.

Writing, on the other hand, isn't a "duo act." When you say something, it represents your individual thoughts, and you can't rely on a partner to interject. While it's not impossible to address every counter-opinion as you write, doing so would be too restrictive and dull. That's why, I think, fiction is so compelling. Characters in novels can deviate freely. They can speak of evil, hypocrisy, or madness. Perhaps it's time to further explore these possibilities.
