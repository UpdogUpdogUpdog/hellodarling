Once upon a time, back then was simply the present.  
Shigesato Itoi

・Over the years, I've had the chance to meet many seniors and hear stories about their "olden days." Listening to them, I often realized that even though people nowadays think of those times in a certain way, the perspective of someone who actually lived through them was often vastly different. I found these stories precious and fascinating. But before I knew it, I found myself on the other side, being the one asked to share my stories. After all, I do have knowledge of things that happened over half a century ago (for someone born in the Showa era, 60 years prior was the Edo period). That's how much time has passed.

Yesterday, the news reported that "Shibuya Seibu" is closing. Since it opened in 1968, I’ve known it since then. I worked on advertisements and various events there. What I've seen and know has now become "stories of the past." So, when I talk about Shibuya Seibu, to young people, it likely sounds like a tale from long ago. Such occurrences have become quite frequent. Even when they react with surprise, exclaiming, "Wow, really?" or lean in wanting to hear more, I can’t help but feel a bit uneasy. Perhaps it's because both the listener and I are living in the same present of 2026. At the very least, I feel that I'm breathing the same air as them, in the same era. I’m quite confident in this feeling. Compared to some younger folks, I believe I'm living more in the present. It's not about wanting to seem young; it's simply the truth as I see it.

Even when I recount things from the past, I speak of them as if discussing the present. It's not about glorifying the past, just stating facts. The present, too, will someday become the past. The past was once merely the present. What changes is the accumulation of years. Yet, I remain as childlike as ever. Everyone, remember: the things we experience and feel today will eventually be shared as stories of the past.

Thank you for visiting "Hobonichi" again today. Living is about creating memories. Today, we make more.
