Eating Biang Biang Noodles  
by Shigesato Itoi

・I'm not particularly a gourmet, nor do I consider myself exceptionally solitary, yet I often find myself dining alone. Sometimes I venture far, other times I stay close. If it's a Saturday or Sunday and the journey is within an hour by train, I might just set out. Well, this happens occasionally. More often than not, my destination is a ramen shop. Though I can't claim to compete with the ramen enthusiasts, I frequently watch the BS-TBS show, "Eating Ramen." When I travel a long distance for ramen, it tastes delicious even before I've taken a bite. I go with the expectation of deliciousness, and so it is. If the shop were nearby, it might not feel the same, but the journey adds to the flavor. "Eating" begins with "going to eat," and before "going," there is the "imagining."

With that in mind, I've long wanted to try "Biang Biang Noodles." The "biang biang" part does have a character, but it's so complex that it can't be rendered here. It supposedly has 50 to 60 strokes—though that's not set in stone—and was apparently created specifically for "Biang Biang Noodles." The character often becomes a trivia topic, yet no one around here seems to have tried the noodles themselves. People are eager to explain the character but not the dish. Then, one day, I discovered there was a "Biang Biang Noodles" shop right in my own neighborhood, in Kanda Jimbocho. It was so close that I thought I could go anytime, and I left it at that. But recently, by chance, I stumbled upon the exact location of the shop. Seize the day, as they say—I went yesterday! But I won't tell you whether the noodles were thick or thin. When I entered and asked the menu, "Which one is the Biang Biang Noodles?" the response was, "All of these." Neither the character nor the noodles will be explained by me, but leaving it a mystery won't affect your life in the slightest (laugh).
