・Let's start with a quote.  
　This might be the first time I've ever done something like this.  
　The source is "Aozora Bunko," so you can read the full text afterwards.  
　(I've enclosed kana in brackets and added a few line breaks.)

　"In the autumn of 1902, on an unspecified day,  
　I waved a white flag from the window of my bedroom  
　and surrendered to the landlady.  
　The landlady, weighing in at twenty kan,  
　began the arduous climb to the top of the three-story building.  
　I say 'began' rather than 'climbed' to emphasize the effort involved.  
　There are precisely 42 steps to ascend,  
　requiring two rest stops along the way.  
　After spending three minutes and five seconds,  
　this formidable landlady's face appeared pained at the doorway,  
　while the surroundings felt claustrophobically narrow.  
　As she stood there, towering and imposing,  
　she authoritatively conveyed the first terms of our truce:  
　'You must ride a bicycle.'"

　The distinction between periods and commas might be unclear,  
　and though it seems complicated, this passage is brimming with humor.  
　It's the opening of Natsume Sōseki's essay "Bicycle Diary."  
　He mixes grandiose prose with a storytelling style reminiscent of rakugo,  
　as if saying, "Feast on the exquisite technique of my writing."  
　This tone continues throughout.  
　In short, it's a tale of being compelled to ride a bicycle,  
　but no matter how he tries, he can't and he's had enough.  
　It's written with the craftsmanship of a skilled artisan,  
　like the intricate carvings in a brand-new house.  
　After all, this is Natsume Sōseki, a great figure, a man on our currency,  
　a literary giant of the Meiji era.  
　Perhaps he could write this with one hand,  
　scribbling lazily as if doodling in half-sleep.  
　But I don't think that's the case.  
　I believe he wrote with sincere dedication, even in play or hobby.  
　Not that I have any particular reflections to share.
