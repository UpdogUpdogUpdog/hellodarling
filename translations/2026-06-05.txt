Health is precious, and being okay is truly a blessing.  
By Shigesato Itoi

Lately, I've been reflecting deeply on how important it is to be "healthy." In Kenji Miyazawa’s poem "Ame ni mo Makezu," there's a line about "having a robust body." This poem encapsulates Miyazawa's vision of an ideal human being, and "health" is undeniably a significant element of that vision.

Being "healthy" isn't quite the same as being "strong." You can be healthy without necessarily being strong. It’s more about resilience, like being resistant to illness or not breaking down easily. If we think of it in terms of offense and defense, it's more about the latter. It's about not falling apart. At first glance, it might not seem like a big deal, but even strong things can be fragile and break. Nowadays, I find myself valuing more those who may seem weak but refuse to break down.

In "Ame ni mo Makezu," the person who is "called a fool by everyone, neither praised nor troubled" is, after all, set up to be "healthy." There's something profoundly important about that.

I started wondering about the origins of the word "healthy." It piqued my curiosity to look it up. "丈" is a unit of length, and "夫" means a husband or an adult male. When combined, it makes sense: a man of stature. A respectable, fully-fledged man. So, the qualities that such a man possesses define "health," perhaps. I'm not entirely sure if I get it, but that's okay.

And a particularly admirable man is "大丈夫"—which means "okay" or "fine." So that's why "大丈夫" means "okay." Again, not entirely clear, but that's fine too. Trying to dig into it too much made me lose the nuance. Anyway, what I really wanted to say is that I've come to appreciate the importance of being "healthy." As long as I'm "healthy," I feel like I can be "okay."

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nichi" today. If we manage to stay "healthy," we can prepare for whatever comes next.
