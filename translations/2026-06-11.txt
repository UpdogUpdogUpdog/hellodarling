The Labor of Cowboys  
Shigesato Itoi

・Every day, pondering "What should I eat today?" might feel like a hassle to some, but it's a privilege, really. The mere act of choosing is something remarkable.

There used to be an American TV series called "Rawhide," back when Clint Eastwood was still a young actor. It was a story about cowboys on a journey, herding cattle, and their meals were bean dishes every single day. I think it was a stew of salted pork and green beans. The cowboys would grumble, "Beans again, day after day," and Wishbone, the old cook, would retort with his usual, "If you don't want it, don't eat it!" As a grade schooler, I found those exchanges amusing. I've held onto this comedy-like banter throughout the years, often recalling it even as an adult. It speaks to the hardship of having nothing but the same meal every day.

The word "COWBOY" literally means "cattle driver." These are workers who transport thousands of cattle as their job. They're not the dashing figures spinning guns or roping in outlaws with a lasso, wearing ten-gallon hats. It's an occupation where they ride horses through rain and wind, moving herds of cattle. And they eat nothing but beans every day. That's something we modern folks couldn't possibly emulate; it's a tremendous feat. It parallels the era of the transcontinental railroad, too. The brief history of the vast United States is also a narrative of such arduous pioneering labor.

I might sound preachy, but being able to say "What should I eat today?" is built upon the muddy tales of those who moved cattle while eating nothing but beans. Are you listening, me? I find myself thinking, now more than ever, that we must not forget a kind of respect for such history. No matter how much information increases or AI advances, without a foundation in reality, it's all just like clouds. Someone has to prepare the water, the beans, the cattle, and the roads.

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nikkan" today. If America has a history of pioneering, then what kind of history does Japan have?
