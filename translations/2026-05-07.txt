The curious form of expression known as the memo.  
Shigesato Itoi

・"I'll write more after I've given it some thought."  
   There are memos I jot down with that intention.  
   Sometimes, I do actually think it through and write more.  
   Other times, I think it over but end up leaving the memo as is, unable to write further.  
   It makes me feel a touch inadequate, like I lack the power to think deeply.  
   But lately, I've started to wonder if that's really the case.

   Back when I was a copywriter, I wrote a tagline like "Delicious Life."  
   Beyond that, I penned a few lines of body copy, but it was neither an essay nor lengthy.  
   If someone had told me to "elaborate more" back then,  
   would I have been able to go on and on explaining that tagline?  
   I was interviewed about it a few times, but I could never quite explain it to anyone's satisfaction.  
   Looking back now, I think that's how it should have been.  
   Consider haiku, tanka, or brief poetry.  
   There's the famous poem "An old pond / A frog jumps in / The sound of water."  
   It doesn't need any body copy, nor does it need to be expanded into a thesis.  
   Of course, if the poet wishes, they can speak about it as much as they like.  
   Isn't it something like that?

   Fully aware that this might be self-serving,  
   I've come to think that even the short sentences of a memo, though not an art form,  
   might be just fine as they are.  
   Whether to elaborate further on what's written,  
   you can if you want to, but you can also present it just as it is.

   ◆The value of freedom,  
   how truly wonderful it is,  
   is evident in the fact that "depriving someone of freedom is a punishment."  
   ...This is a memo noted as is.  
   Continuing it further seems like it would just become tiresome.

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nikkan Itoi Shinbun" today.  
That, too, might have been captured in a shorter memo.
