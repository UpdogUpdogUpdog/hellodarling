I Want to Hold You Tight  
by Shigesato Itoi

・It's one of those things you might say, "Why bring it up now?" but the feeling of "What is this?!" when The Beatles first appeared is something I could never forget, even if I tried. Especially the song "I Want to Hold Your Hand"—that one left a deep mark.

In the book "Happy Ending" by Kenta Hagiwara, Eiichi Ohtaki says, "When I heard 'I Want to Hold Your Hand' on FEN, it was like a jolt of electricity. I knew it, this was it!" You can see how this song had a special impact in Ohtaki's own personal "great history of music."

That said, let's talk about the song title. We casually say "I Want to Hold You Tight," but both Ohtaki and I knew the original title, "I Want to Hold Your Hand." We were content with "I Want to Hold You Tight," but The Beatles' lyrics aren't saying that. Despite the song’s powerful music and performance, the lyrics are more like those of an innocent high schooler. "I want to hold your hand," they say. "I've got something to tell you," they say. "I can't hide these feelings," they say. Isn't that so modest?

The intro goes JAJAJAAAAN! It's an iconic JAJAJAAAAN like Beethoven's "Fate," a sound that echoes through history. And then, to say "I want to hold your hand" feels a bit weak, doesn't it? If the single's jacket had "I Want to Hold Your Hand" printed on it back then, I think it would have looked like some kind of "youth pop" thing. But someone over at Toshiba Records must have thought, "Sure, they're saying they want to hold hands, but deep down they're shouting 'I want to hold you tight!'" and presented it to Japan that way. They broke through the British gentlemanly reserve with a bit of Japanese boldness. I'm glad they went with "I Want to Hold You Tight." Because deep down, everyone wants to hold each other tight, don't they?

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nichi" today. I wonder if the expression "I want to hold you tight" existed before that song?
