The Work of a Copywriter  
Shigesato Itoi

At one point, I decided to quit being an advertising copywriter. Since it's a job you can claim just by saying you do it, announcing that I "quit" might seem odd, but I stopped taking on projects from clients. Back then, whenever my title mentioned "copywriter," I'd quickly add, "I'm not doing that anymore; just call me the head of Hobonichi."

Eventually, I got tired of explaining it every time. I figured if people still saw me as a copywriter, I might as well let them. I also started to see what I was doing as similar to copywriting, only now the requests came from "us."

The term "copywriter" was once translated into Japanese as "advertising writer." It's the job of creating "advertising copy." In the context of "copy and concept," the "copy" tends to stand out, while the "concept" is less tangible and harder to define. As a result, people often see it as someone who writes cleverly. In the Edo and Meiji periods, advertising copy likely existed as a form of "playful writing."

However, times have changed, and although the expression remains "copy," the role of "concept" has grown more significant. As "concept" expands and deepens, what's required becomes "conceptualization." Though translated as "concept," it's more about saying, "When you look at it this way, this is what it is." Naturally, concepts can hit or miss. For instance, trying to conceptualize sumo wrestlers as a "troupe of historical drama athletes" is possible, though not particularly practical. So, if coming up with concepts is a copywriter's job, then I've been crafting concepts for Hobonichi all along. (Will this continue?)

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi today. I think there's a different kind of training for the "copy" rather than the "concept."
