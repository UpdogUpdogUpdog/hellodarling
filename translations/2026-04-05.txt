There are days when I just can't settle down. Calm yourself.

In the middle of the night, I scrapped a draft I'd already finished. So now, I have to write something new from scratch. That's just part of being an adult, little one. I’ve already admitted this publicly, but I’m someone who dislikes and struggles with writing drafts. The thought of having to start anew is just exasperating. If that’s the case, why did I scrap the first one, you ask? Well, I already made up my mind. Why did I discard it? Because I wrote it in fits and starts, trying too hard to make it coherent, and it ended up being completely uninteresting. Not that everything I write is always riveting, but when even I can outright say "this is boring," it must be pretty bad.

Alright, let's switch gears and write as if this were a diary entry.

Yesterday, Masahiko Sato gave a lecture. It was held at the Yasuda Auditorium at the University of Tokyo. The title, "Creating Ways to Create," was already intriguing. It was a long session, spanning two 80-minute classes, but as expected of Sato, a researcher of communication, he managed to keep us from getting bored while clearly conveying his message. The entire time was fascinating. For those who were keen to absorb the information, it must have been truly beneficial. The lecture was both "enjoyable" and "useful," making it an ideal event. Masahiko Sato has been researching these kinds of topics for a long time, continuously evolving his ideas, nurturing others, while keeping himself engaged—what an admirable feat. I, too, was soaking up all of Sato's insights as a particularly eager student. "Continuously doing interesting things, continuously," I realized there's nothing more wonderful than that.

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nichi" today. Having too many "half-baked thoughts" isn't good, is it?
