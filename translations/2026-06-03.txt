I've stumbled upon a delightful discovery: a brilliant example of "things you don't need to do."

When I write, I rarely use quotes. Well, I do use them sometimes, but not often. Why? Because it's a hassle. I prefer to work with whatever's lying around, to make do with what's readily available. If I were to dress it up in intellectual garb, I'd call it a sort of "bricolage." So, if there's a grand quote on hand, it doesn't fit the "available materials" category, and thus it becomes a cumbersome method I'm not fond of. Ironically, just as I was about to pen this thought, I stumbled upon a text so compelling that I couldn't resist quoting it. And, lo and behold, it was right there, part of the stuff lying around.

Sugano said, "You don't have to do what you have to do. (Repeat the same line three times.)" Nagata, who was there, chimed in, "That's right. The things you 'have to do' leave little room for fun." Sugano asked, "Wait, what? Say it again, word for word." Nagata replied, "The things you 'have to do' leave little room for fun. In contrast, the potential for fun in things you don't need to do is limitless. After all, they're things you don't need to do."

What a magnificent conversation! This was a snippet from yesterday. How could I not pick it up when I stumbled upon it? I'm going to swipe it, copy it down without handing it over to the lost and found. It's like a quote, a bit of lost property I've pocketed, something akin to my "bricolage."

Yes, indeed. When you do something out of obligation, the outcome is predictable and lacks excitement. The folks over at "Hobonichi" are always on the lookout for things they don't need to do, wandering around Shinjuku today as well.
