Reflections of the Big Sister Teacher  
Shigesato Itoi

・This past week, I've been traveling to Akagi and back again, and now I've finally returned to Tokyo. My last stop was meeting with Tsunakan in Karakuwa, where we nostalgically caught up, prayed facing the sea, and reassured each other of our well-being. We even jokingly made a pact to attend each other's funerals. Lately, this has become a bit of a ritual. It's like saying, "Don't take gold and me for granted." Truly, with just a slight shift, fate can change dramatically. While it's okay to believe in tomorrow, we shouldn't be too certain about the day after, ten years down the line, or eternity. We must live for today. Forgive me for my knack for saying inauspicious things at just the right moment—it's always been part of my charm.

Ichiyo-san has always said she relies on our boss like an "older brother." But when we met yesterday, she suddenly elevated him to "Kanako-san is like a father." Remarkable, isn't it? Our boss, our lady, and now a father figure. Incidentally, "Itoisan is like an older sister," she says, meaning she can tell me anything. We've transcended societal notions of gender at this point. She even mistakenly calls me "sensei" sometimes, so I guess I'm the "big sister teacher" now. I might as well embrace living as "big sister teacher."

In Kesennuma, I saw so many children. Of course, I pay attention to children in Tokyo too. With age, children seem to shine more brightly to me. While adults are busy lamenting about various despairs, tallying up their pessimism and gloom every day, children, just by being there, embody "the future." Incidentally, AI doesn't "believe" in the future. It doesn't believe but rather hypothesizes that "it will likely exist" based on statistics. That's its own kind of amusing character.

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nikkan" today. I somehow managed to bruise my rib on the armrest of a hotel chair, and it hurts a bit.
