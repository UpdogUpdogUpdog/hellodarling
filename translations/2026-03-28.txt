Things That Exist But Don’t, and Things That Don’t But Do  
by Shigesato Itoi

・Once, when asked, "What do you want to become?" I answered, "I want to become a home run." There were people who read that and said, "That's great." I was a bit pleased by that.

A home run isn't made of atoms, so it doesn't exist as a physical object. It's an event with a name. Yet, many people witness it. There's the player who hit it and the pitcher who gave it up—it certainly happened. It can be recorded, counted one by one. You can even capture it in photos or videos. But if someone told you to put it down here, it's an impossible request. No one owns a home run. Sure, the home run ball is real, and the fact that someone hit it is undeniable.

When you think about it, love is like that too. It's even more complicated than a home run. There's no official record, and it's uncertain whether it even happened. But if both people believe it's there, then it's there (in this case, it can't be just one person who feels this way). And it can't exist as a physical object any more than a home run can. Friendship works in the same way. Even happiness—it's not something you can take out and examine. At best, you can struggle to define it and try to recreate something that matches that definition.

No matter how much you dissect a living thing, you won't find "life" anywhere inside. Yet, the fact that it is alive is undeniable. Dissecting or analyzing won't uncover life. You can record the act of living, and everyone encounters this "life." Come to think of it, life is a lot like a home run. You can create something similar with robots, but it's only something that resembles life. Oh, and live music is called "live," isn't it? "Live" is, essentially, life.
