Thoughts on Roads  
by Shigesato Itoi

・One day, a thought struck me.  
  As I was vaguely staring at a wide road, I found myself thinking, "This road is incredibly wide."  
  If it’s a road with three lanes on each side, that’s a total of six lanes.  
  Add the sidewalks on both sides, and the area is immense.  
  You could fit several small buildings in that space.

  When roads have neither people nor cars on them, they are utterly empty.  
  No one can live there, nor are there any shops.  
  In terms of geometry, roads are akin to lines, and people don’t often think about the area they cover.  
  Roads are just pathways for going back and forth, so we don’t usually consider their vastness.  
  Yet, in reality, they cover a tremendous area.  
  And creating these roads requires an astonishing amount of labor, materials, and money.  
  I mean, I’ve never built a road myself, but it’s easy to imagine how challenging it must be just to construct a single meter of a road of a certain width.  
  Roads in ancient times must have been even more of an ordeal to create.

  Furthermore, whether a road exists or doesn’t, whether it’s passable or not, is immensely important to human society.  
  Like securing water sources, roads have been crucial considerations throughout history.  
  You can’t live on them, build on them, or create from them.  
  Yet maintaining them incurs costs.  
  Roads exist solely for “going back and forth,” but that very essence of movement is life itself.  
  You see, even in our bodies, blood vessels—our blood’s pathways—are intricately spread throughout, both thick and thin.  
  When life ends, that “going back and forth” ceases.  
  (A quick check on ChatGPT tells me that the total length of human blood vessels is about 100,000 kilometers—enough to circle the Earth two and a half times.)  
  Roads are often used as metaphors for various things, and rightly so—they are truly remarkable.  
  Traveling back and forth on existing roads is important, but creating new roads where there were none and forging ahead is equally extraordinary.  
  I get why my dad would sing "My Way" with such feeling.
