Is the idea of becoming an adult at 30 too easygoing?  
By Shigesato Itoi

・The times are becoming increasingly relentless.  
What used to be considered "common sense" now seems not just outdated, but so leisurely it might even be criticized for being too laid-back.  
When words like "cost performance" and "time performance" started popping up in everyday conversation, I found myself wondering, "Is this really how it is now?"  
Perhaps the era has evolved to a point where questioning the validity of cost and time efficiency is itself seen as passé.  
Historically, declaring something "old-fashioned" has always been a way to stake a claim on what's new, hasn't it?

With that preamble, I find myself reflecting on the notion of "adulthood at 30."  
I still subscribe to this idea, and I think others have mentioned it too. Essentially, while the law considers you an adult at 20, in today's society, your twenties feel like an extension of childhood.  
The concept suggests that truly becoming an adult by 30 might be more appropriate. Back then, the term "moratorium" was often used negatively, but the "adulthood at 30" theory flipped it on its head, presenting it positively.  
Long ago, it was expected that one would mature and work independently at a young age, but with society growing more complex and lifespans extending, perhaps deciding on adulthood at 20 is rushing things.  
Personally, I didn't feel like an adult until around 30. I've often told younger people that it's okay to be more foolish in their twenties. Failures and mistakes included, the ideas and actions during that time lay the groundwork for the long years ahead.

Yet, the reality is that the age of adulthood has been lowered to 18. It feels like there's no time for foolishness anymore. In this increasingly demanding era, isn't the idea of becoming an adult at 30 actually more important than ever?
