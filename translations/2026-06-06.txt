There aren't that many things I can really answer.

As a child, and even when I was young, whenever an adult would ask me, "What do you want to be when you grow up?" I honestly had no idea. When my friends talked about which singers or actors from TV or movies they liked, I didn't have a clue about that either. It felt like I had to decide on something and give an answer. Even though I never truly knew, I would mull over it and somehow settle on something. It was an answer I forced myself to make.

In reality, it's not always possible to have an answer ready. This holds true not just for kids, but for adults too.

For every little thing, for every topic, it's impossible to have a ready opinion or to put everything into words. It's not a matter of being able to "verbalize" it. Often, I don't even have an opinion at all. If someone says I'm insensitive, maybe they're right. If asked whether I feel anything, the answer might be no, or maybe yes.

There are feelings I have that even I don't recognize, and sometimes it takes years, a long time later, to understand what they were. This happens a lot with relationships, too. For people who were very close to me, like my father or mother, I feel like I never really understood what I felt or thought about them. Whether I liked or disliked them, felt gratitude or resentment—I never truly knew.

People aren't meant to have all the answers to every question. Is there really anyone out there who can? We don't know, not about others, and not about ourselves. Living is about feeling and realizing things, even if we don't have answers. We ask such difficult questions so easily, expecting simple answers.

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nikkan" today. Yesterday, too, there was so much I didn't understand, and it was a lot of fun.
