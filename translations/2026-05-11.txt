Do You Favor a .500 Win Rate?  
Shigesato Itoi

・A .500 win rate means breaking even—no savings, no debt. No matter how many games you play, wins and losses are perfectly balanced. It's never been a particularly inspiring image. Especially in sports, a .500 win rate won't get you a championship. So, when my favorite team hovered around that mark, I used to feel a pang of disappointment, thinking, "They're just not cutting it."

But lately, perhaps as I've matured, I've started to think, "Isn't a .500 win rate not so bad?" Having the same number of wins and losses could, in a rather cynical way, mean "whether you play or not, it doesn't matter."

However, I've realized that it’s also the exact state of "Ready, set, go!" With no savings and no debt, starting something new is just like being at .500. Incidentally, if you've played a lot and still have no savings or debt, it means you've gained "experience." And during that time, you've lived and kept moving.

To be honest, as a mere sports enthusiast, I'm not satisfied with a .500 win rate. I can easily say I love a team that dominates the league. That's true, but I also want to cultivate more respect for the .500 win rate. Like a team with a .500 win rate that you can't help but cheer for, or one that seems ready to break out, or a team with great camaraderie despite their record. If you can feel or help others feel these things, I think the enjoyment multiplies.

Well, sports are a stand-in for battles, so naturally, winning and losing are important. But in other fields, finding success with an appealing .500 win rate would be quite something.
