Anticipation of the Cherry Blossoms

・The time is almost upon us. Yes, it's that season when cherry blossoms bloom—spring. Every year, right around the end of February, just after Setsubun, I find myself shivering in the cold, yet thinking how soon this chill will be a distant memory. It’s then that the saying “spring is not far” comes to mind. We all know that warmth will surely return, and the opening of cherry blossoms is the sign of that promise. A time when everywhere you look, the world bursts into vivid color.

The trees at Aoyama Cemetery, now dark and bare, will soon be lively with blossoms. On my way to work, I see the moat by the Imperial Palace, the tree-lined paths leading to Nippon Budokan, and the banks of Chidorigafuchi, all turning cherry pink as if for some grand celebration. Such a boisterous spring is on its way. As I walk or drive, I picture the cherry blossom season I’ve always admired. But they haven’t bloomed yet, though they will soon. Spring is coming, inevitable and unstoppable. While waiting, I’m filled with anticipation, but even before the first hint of blooms, I imagine the blossoms coming and going, and a sense of loneliness creeps in. Another year has gone by, once again.

Lately, each day as I pass through Daikancho, I find myself checking on the cherry trees. And now, the news programs have started talking about the "bloom forecasts." They’re discussing when the cherries at Yasukuni Shrine will blossom. It seems that day is drawing near. In a few days, we’ll likely see footage of adorable buds. Even though it's still cold now, soon the cherry blossoms will bloom, and spring will arrive. It’s something to look forward to, yet it brings sadness too. Last year, the year before that, always waiting for the cherry blossoms with both anticipation and a touch of melancholy. And when they finally bloom, I start thinking about the day they’ll fall, feeling even more wistful, and also looking forward to summer.

Thank you for visiting “Hobo Nikkan” today. While waiting, there’s not just anticipation, but also a tinge of loneliness.
