Words Picked Up by New York Magazine  
by Shigesato Itoi

Recently, I was interviewed by a media outlet called "New York Magazine." They've covered my work before, and I remember feeling quite pleased about it. This time, however, it was a remote interview. The conversation we had in mid-April has already been published. The journalist, a long-time user of the Hobonichi Techo, was incredibly knowledgeable, which made the introduction both lively and profound. It was a joy to read.

After reading the entire piece, I noticed the title was prominently displayed:  
We Designed the Hobonichi Techo  
"It's like a park for children where you have space to just play around and have fun with it each day."  
This was something I said during the interview. It was a spontaneous remark, the kind where you hope the listener understands what you mean. In Japan, I've given many interviews about the Hobonichi Techo, but this is the first time such an expression became the title. "It's like a park for children, where you have space to just play and enjoy yourself every day," I did indeed say that. But I wondered, would such a description resonate with someone unfamiliar with the Hobonichi Techo? It made me realize why the Hobonichi Techo is so popular overseas—they truly grasped the essence of it.

More than listing the benefits or emphasizing the product's quality, saying, "It's like a park for children where you have space to enjoy yourself," might convey our intentions more effectively. Learning this gave me a great deal of courage. I want to express various things more freely. I want to think of good ideas "like playing" more than ever before. Well, at this moment, I am here.

Thank you for visiting Hobonichi today. And to those reading from across the distant seas, thank you.
