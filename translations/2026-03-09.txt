A Time Machine to the World of Fourteen-Year-Olds  
Shigesato Itoi

・Even if I introduce this film, it won't ensure that everyone will have the chance to see it. If destiny plays a part, if you're lucky, or if you pursue it with fervor, then perhaps you'll get to watch it. Currently, the number of cinemas showing it is limited, though you might find it in places other than traditional theaters. However, it's not a loss if you miss it. The film doesn't contain anything particularly rare; it's just about 35 ordinary eighth graders living their lives. It's as if you are right there among them. Yet, those ordinary eighth graders, in their very ordinariness, are all incredibly (to use a trendy term) "precious."

The film's title refers to a documentary that listens to the stories of a class of 35 students at a certain school, capturing their daily lives. While each has their own narrative, the film doesn't artificially add depth or drama to their stories. Every one of the 35 students is covered to roughly the same extent, so even if you want to know more about them, you can't pursue it further. It simply captures about two months leading up to their advancement to the next grade when spring arrives. The camera visits classrooms, school routes, and occasionally their homes, maintaining a comfortable familiarity with the fourteen-year-olds. For each of these fourteen-year-olds, nothing has truly begun, and nothing has truly ended. Every day, the present piles up like snow falling and settling.

Naturally, every viewer will be prompted to reflect on their own fourteen-year-old self. I found myself thinking, "Maybe I was even more childish," as I boarded this time machine, spending about two hours exploring the world of fourteen-year-olds, wide-eyed. The world shown to me by these 35 ordinary kids felt, in a good way, much more old-fashioned and genuine than the world of the internet we casually browse today. It struck me—"Living as a person" is quite earnest and, in a way, very old-fashioned—and that realization brought me comfort. I'm grateful to the team behind this film and to these fourteen-year-olds.

Thank you for visiting "Hobo Nikkan" again today.
