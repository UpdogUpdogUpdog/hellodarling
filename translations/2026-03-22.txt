Bringing More "Oh, I See" Moments to the World  
by Shigesato Itoi

Visiting Kuma-chan in Nara is genuinely delightful. Even though I was right there, part of the conversation, now that time has passed, I find myself enjoying it as a reader. Apparently, this series will continue for over 30 more installments, which makes me happy. We certainly talked a lot, so it makes sense it stretches out like this.

I've had a friendly rapport with Shinbo Minami since the very first time we met. It's visible in everything about him; it's as if you can just tell. But with Katsuyuki Shinohara, known as Kuma-chan, you'd think it might have been intimidating at first. He's a man who seems to be from the toughest of the tough crowd, the kind who would appear frightening at a glance. However, that wasn't the case. From the start, he was naturally warm and welcoming.

Kuma-chan spent his days in Shinjuku's Golden Gai, a place infamous for its constant arguments and fights, working as a sort of bouncer for the notoriously rough Kara Jūrō. Knowing his role, it's easy to imagine him as intimidating. Yet, up close, he was genuinely a pleasant person. In hindsight, people might understand this now, but back then, there was a real intensity to his presence.

Being with Kuma-chan brings a sense of relief. It's as if there's a broad sense of acceptance, a willingness to embrace everything in a positive way. Whether it's a trivial matter or a serious discussion, he effortlessly absorbs it with a simple "Oh, I see." Kuma-chan's "Oh, I see" is really something special. First, there's the "Oh," that moment of realization. Then, the "I see," a sign of understanding and acceptance. Added to this is Kuma-chan's unique flavor: a hidden "Well, maybe it doesn't matter all that much." I don't think I know anyone else who can say "Oh, I see" quite like that.

If husbands and wives, parents and children, and even strangers could incorporate more "Oh, I see" into their interactions, I believe world peace might just inch a little closer. Of course, some people might see this kind of tolerance as careless and despise it, but the unique flavor of being human lies in these very moments.

Thank you for visiting "Almost Daily" today. "Iki-aji" ... a neat new term I just came up with.
