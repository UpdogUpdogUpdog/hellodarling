Things I Do, Things I Don't Do, and Things I Started But Couldn't Finish  
by Shigesato Itoi

・When you really see something through to the end or keep at it over time, people tend to think that's quite an achievement. I have my own examples of this. For instance, I've been writing this "Almost Daily" column consistently for nearly 28 years now without taking a break.

On the flip side, there are countless things that I've never even attempted. That's just how it is—there are far more things I haven't done. Take golf, for example. I haven’t played it. I was invited by a friend to hit the greens once. After that, I never felt the urge to play again. I've been invited a couple more times and even been given a set of clubs, but I never actually played. The same goes for mahjong. I'd hang out in rooms where my friends played, but I'd just read a book instead of joining in. I've played a few times in my life, but I don't have any fond memories of it, nor do I feel any desire to play. Golf and mahjong are just obvious examples, but there are countless things I haven't done. Among them are things I’ve deliberately decided against doing.

Then there's a third category besides things I do and things I don't—things I started but couldn't continue. In my case, this list is surprisingly long. Let's start with something you might not know about: the shamisen. I once thought it would be fun to play the musical introduction to a rakugo performance myself, so I bought one in Kyoto and even found a teacher to take lessons. After just one lesson, I realized it was hopeless for me. On days when the teacher was supposed to come, I'd sneak out the back door to avoid them. I’ve given up on every musical instrument, and any athletic pursuits usually end after the second attempt. With languages and other lessons, I get cold feet right after deciding to start. Just the other day, I even gave up on sunbathing because of rain and strong winds. I sometimes wonder why I even try these things, but every now and then there's something like fishing or this "Almost Daily" column that I end up genuinely enjoying and sticking with for a long time.

Thank you for visiting "Almost Daily" again today. You could say that even giving up after three days is better than not trying at all.
