In the dead of night, I ventured out to the postbox to send a postcard.  
Shigesato Itoi

・Before the date could change from May 14th,  
　I slipped on my sandals and walked to the nearby mailbox.  
　It wasn't for anything else, just to mail a postcard.

　Just moments ago, I had been at the venue for Kenji Ozawa's "Aidate of the Moon and City,"  
　and I had only just returned home.  
　At the end of the concert, he had encouraged us,  
　"Please write something—anything—and send it."  
　He said he found it particularly delightful to read the handwritten text on a postcard,  
　rather than digital fonts on the internet.  
　He explained, "If you're in the city, it’ll arrive in a day,"  
　and "You don't even need to put a stamp on it; the address is already printed."  
　He urged us repeatedly, "Please send a postcard."

　The concert was truly magnificent.  
　A kaleidoscope of schemes and playful ideas had been prepared with utmost sincerity,  
　and the stage setup, the performance, the singing, all of it was extraordinary in both quality and quantity.  
　Though many things I wish to convey will surely be discussed by others elsewhere,  
　what impressed me the most was the "strong and resilient energy"  
　that seemed to drive the conveyance of all those things.  
　Kenji Ozawa is, in a sense, being reborn.  
　I felt a joy in sharing such a moment in time.  
　As for the rest, each audience member had been given a set,  
　which included a postcard and a pencil, with instructions to write and send.  
　With so much urging, I decided I would certainly send a postcard.

　And so, the story circles back to the beginning.  
　It might be a joy to read them, but it feels equally good to write them.  
　Scrawling down how wonderful it was in haste,  
　and then dashing to the mailbox in the middle of the night was an unparalleled thrill.  
　People like me, probably hundreds, will have sent postcards.  
　Imagining Kenji Ozawa reading them all,  
　I thought, what a wonderfully enviable ending to a concert.
